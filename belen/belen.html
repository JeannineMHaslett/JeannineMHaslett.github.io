Welcome to the Hub City

Belen, New Mexico. Established 1740

The city was originally named Nuestra Senora de Belen (Our Lady of Belen), and the Catholic Church in the center of town still bears this name. Belen means Bethlehem in Spanish. The town of Belen was named after the birthplace of Jesus due to the fact that the climate is similar to the climate of Palestine.

The original cowby (vaquero) culture developed along the Rio Grande, which passes through Belen. It was brought here by colonists of Don Juan De OÃ±ate who arrived in 1598. The colonists were ranchers, and their way of life developed into what people now know as cowboys.

Belen New Mexico was part of a 200,000 acre Spanish land grant (called the Rio Abajo, or lower river) from King Charles(Carlos) III of Spain on November 15, 1740 given to Captain Diego Torres. Descendants of Torres still reside in Belen. In 1740, Mexico was considered a colony of Spain, and New Mexico was part of Mexico. The land grant stretched from the Rio Puerco on the west side, to the Manzano mountains on the east side, from Tome and across the Rio Grande to Los Chavez on the north and down to the town of Bernardo on the south.

The Manzano mountains on the east side of Belen


The Camino Real (Royal Road) passes directly through Belen. The Camino Real was a trade route that stretched 1,590 miles between Mexico City and San Juan Pueblo in northern New Mexico. It has been deemed a historic landmark. Travelers can follow the historic road which is marked with information signs along the road. Highway 314 (which used to be highway 85) runs from South Belen to Albuquerque. The road signs can be found along this route. for more information, visit National Historic Trail NM, National Park Service

After the Mexican-American war, the treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848 the United States acquired much of the Southwest. Shortly thereafter, a variety of European families settled in Belen. Many of our streets around parks are named after the original settlers. There are German names like Sachs, Becker, Dalies and Reinken, and French names like Feil and Didier.

In 1880, the railroad came to New Mexico and the Santa Fe Railroad was born. Belen, being directly in the center of the state, was an excellent place for trains to connect. This created a booming economy for Belen, as commodities were traded, and visitors stopped to eat and sleep. Belen became what was known as a railroad hub, and thus, Belen was nicknamed the Hub City. A Harvey House was built at the railiroad station. A Harvey House was a place where the railroad engiineers could sleep. There was a restaurant there for people to enjoy a meal on their commute. The women who worked at the Harvey House were called Harvey Girls. The railroad was good for the hospitality industry in Belen.

Our Movie Industry

Belen's Old Western feel with our old hotels, Harvey House, tumbleweeds, trains and dust is a magnet for production companies looking to make movies. Belen has seen its fair share of celebrities in recent years. the Movie Industry keeps many Belenites employed and ranchers with horses, cattle, sheep and goats are in high demand. Extras can make up to $80 a day, as well. With Netflix moving into Albuquerque, Belen is becoming a well-known town for moviemakers. The most recent movie made in Belen was "Cry Macho", by Clint Eastwood.

Built in 1907 to serve travelers from the railroad, the Hotel Belen has been in a number of movies.

Livelihoods

Farming

Much of Belen is open space and rural. Because of this, farming is one of the things that sustains residents. Many people grow hay and alfalfa, which is bailed and sold to feed cattle and horses. There are also several small dairies in the area.

Field of alfalfa in Los Chavez, north of Belen.


Restaurants

Belen has no shortage of family-owned restaurants. Most of them serve New Mexican food--the kind of Mexican food that can only be found in the Middle Rio Grande Valley. Central New Mexican food has a strong Native American influence, which comes with a flavor all its own.

Pete's Cafe on First Street near the Harvey House Museum and train depot.

Pete Torres, a descendent of Captain Diego Torres who got the Belen Land Grant in 1740, owns and operates Pet's Cafe. Pete's has been serving Mexican food to Residents of Belen since 1949.


Auto Repair

Color Me Crazy Hot Rods entries at the Supernationals car show.

There are many auto repair shops in the Belen area. Color me Crazy Hot Rods is an autobody repair and restoration business. Owner Jimmy Hardyman has won a number of trophies with the cars he has restored and entered in the Supernationals car shows.

Currently Happening

Circle K moving to a new location

The Circle K on Main Street and Aragon will be getting a new building. It will be moving into the empty lot across Aragon. Construction is going quickly. The new store will allow for more gas pumps and getting in and out of the area will be much easier.

The new Circle K being built on Main Street South of Aragon

The old Circle K building was actually a 7-11 that was built in 1960. When 7-11 left Belen, Circle K moved into the building. The now 60-year-old building is in much need of repair. There is very little room for parking and there are only two gas pumps. The only entrance in is on the east or west and there is very little room for maneuvering, especially after main street was widened. The new building is a welcome sight for residents.

The old Circle K building on Main Street north of Aragon.
